# Spain Travel Guide: Regions, Routes and Practical Tips

Spain is a country where ancient history meets vibrant modern culture, where Mediterranean beaches give way to snow-capped mountains, and where regional identities run as deep as the roots of centuries-old olive groves. This **Spain travel guide** covers everything you need to know about exploring this diverse nation—from the architectural marvels of Catalonia to the flamenco-filled streets of Andalusia.

## Key Takeaways

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| **What is the best time to visit?** | **Spring (April-May) and fall (September-October)** offer ideal weather and fewer crowds than summer. June is warm but increasingly busy. |
| **How much does it cost?** | Budget travelers: €40-60/day; mid-range: €80-150/day; luxury: €200+/day. Prices vary by region and season. |
| **How do I get there?** | Fly into major hubs like Madrid, Barcelona, or Málaga. Spain has excellent **public transport** via trains and buses between regions. |
| **Is it safe?** | Spain is generally very safe. Petty theft in tourist areas is the main concern—keep valuables secure and stay aware in crowded places. |
| **What should I book in advance?** | Book [flights](https://trip.tpo.lv/TmObooZ5?subid=blog) 2-3 months ahead; reserve major attractions like the Alhambra and Sagrada Familia weeks in advance; accommodation during peak season. |
| **Do I need a visa?** | EU/EEA citizens need only a passport. US, Canadian, Australian, and most other nationalities get 90 days visa-free under the Schengen agreement. |
| **What currency is used?** | The Euro (€). Most places accept cards, but carry cash for small vendors and rural areas. |

## 1. Understanding Spain's Regions: A Geographic and Cultural Overview

Spain is divided into 17 autonomous communities, each with distinct character, cuisine, and traditions. Understanding these regions is essential for planning a meaningful **Spain travel [experience](https://getyourguide.tpo.lv/GuAFfGGK?subid=blog)** that goes beyond the typical tourist trail. The country spans from the Atlantic coast in the northwest to the Mediterranean in the east, with the central plateau and mountain ranges creating dramatic geographic variety.

The northern regions—including [Basque Country](https://go2-spain.com/region/basque-country/), Galicia, and Asturias—are known for green landscapes, seafood-focused cuisine, and strong regional identities. The central plateau (Castile) contains the capital [Madrid travel guide](https://go2-spain.com/city/madrid/) and historic cities like Toledo and Salamanca. The east features the Mediterranean coast and [Catalonia](https://go2-spain.com/region/catalonia/), home to Barcelona and modernist architecture. [Andalusia](https://go2-spain.com/region/andalusia/) in the south showcases Moorish heritage, white villages, and the gateway to Africa.

> **Did You Know?** Spain has more UNESCO World Heritage Sites than any other country in the world except Italy and China, with 49 inscribed properties recognized for their cultural and natural significance.
>
> *Source: [UNESCO World Heritage Centre](https://whc.unesco.org/en/statesparties/es/)*

### The Major Regions at a Glance

**Catalonia** centers on [Barcelona](https://go2-spain.com/city/barcelona/), a cosmopolitan city known for the [Sagrada Familia](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/sagrada-familia/) basilica, [Park Güell](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/park-guell/), and vibrant neighborhoods. The region extends to the Costa Brava coast, where medieval towns perch above turquoise waters. Our team found that Barcelona's energy is unmatched—the Gothic Quarter's narrow medieval streets contrast sharply with the modernist boulevards of Eixample.

**Andalusia** stretches across southern Spain, encompassing [Seville](https://go2-spain.com/city/seville/) with its flamenco culture and [Alcázar Palace](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/alcazar-seville/), Granada home to the [Alhambra](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/alhambra/), and the white villages (pueblos blancos) of the interior. The region pulses with Moorish history and Spanish passion—visiting in spring reveals wildflower-covered hillsides and perfect temperatures for exploration.

### Regional Specialties and Cuisine

Each region has signature dishes reflecting local ingredients and history. [Basque Country](https://go2-spain.com/region/basque-country/) is celebrated for pintxos (small bites), fresh seafood, and [San Sebastián](https://go2-spain.com/city/san-sebastian/) as a culinary capital. [Galicia](https://go2-spain.com/region/galicia/) offers pulpo a la gallega (octopus) and Albariño wine. Catalonia features escalivada (roasted vegetables) and calcots (spring onions). Andalusia is famous for gazpacho, espetos (grilled fish), and jamón ibérico.

## 2. Madrid: Spain's Beating Heart

[Madrid](https://go2-spain.com/city/madrid/) is Spain's largest city and cultural epicenter, home to world-class museums, royal palaces, and neighborhoods ranging from bohemian to upscale. With over 3 million residents, Madrid pulses with energy—from late-night tapas bars to early-morning café culture. The city's layout is relatively navigable, organized around major plazas and connected by an efficient metro system.

When we explored Madrid in late spring, the city was warm but not yet scorching, with outdoor terraces filling with locals enjoying vermouth and conversation. The city rewards wanderers who venture beyond the obvious tourist zones into residential neighborhoods where authentic Spanish life unfolds.

### Central Madrid: Gran Vía, Sol, and the Royal Quarter

The heart of Madrid radiates from **Puerta del Sol**, a major transit hub and symbolic center. From here, the broad **Gran Vía** boulevard stretches northwest, lined with shops, restaurants, and belle époque architecture. This is where you'll find major department stores and international brands, though prices reflect the premium location.

The **Royal Quarter** (Barrio Real) contains the [Royal Palace of Madrid](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/royal-palace-madrid/), Europe's largest royal palace by floor area, with 3,418 rooms. While the royal family resides elsewhere, the palace is open for tours and hosts state functions. Nearby, the **Cathedral of Santa María la Real de la Almudena** offers stunning views from its dome.

**Museum Triangle** (Triángulo del Arte) is Madrid's cultural powerhouse: the [Prado Museum](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/prado-museum/) houses Spanish masterworks by Goya, Velázquez, and Bosch; the Reina Sofía Museum displays Picasso's Guernica and modern Spanish art; the Thyssen-Bornemisza rounds out the trio with European art spanning centuries. A single day isn't enough—plan at least 2-3 hours per museum.

### Neighborhoods Worth Exploring

**La Latina** is Madrid's oldest quarter, with medieval streets, hidden plazas, and authentic taverns. Sunday morning brings the **El Rastro** flea market, where locals hunt vintage finds and tourists navigate crowded aisles. The neighborhood's narrow alleys hide excellent small restaurants and bars serving traditional Spanish fare.

**Malasaña** is the creative, bohemian heart—vintage shops, street art, independent cafés, and young Madrileños define this neighborhood. It's where to find authentic Spanish indie culture, though gentrification has brought higher prices and chain establishments alongside the original character.

**Salamanca** is Madrid's upscale shopping and residential district, home to designer boutiques on Calle Serrano and affluent locals. It's less touristy than central areas but pricier for food and accommodation. The neighborhood has excellent restaurants and is quieter for sleeping.

**Retiro** centers on the beautiful Retiro Park, a 125-hectare green space with a lake, gardens, and the Crystal Palace. Locals come here to relax, jog, and escape the urban bustle. The park is free to enter and perfect for a morning walk before exploring nearby museums.

### Where to Eat in Madrid

Madrid's food scene ranges from Michelin-starred temples to humble tabernas. Traditional Spanish cuisine dominates—croquetas, jamón ibérico, cocido madrileño (a hearty chickpea stew), and fresh seafood from the coast. Our team found that the best meals often come from small, family-run establishments in residential neighborhoods rather than tourist-focused restaurants.

**Budget-friendly options:** Mercado de San Miguel is a famous food market with standing-room bars serving jamón, seafood, and wine—perfect for grazing but touristy and pricey. Local mercados (markets) in neighborhoods like Malasaña offer better value for fresh produce and prepared foods.

**Mid-range dining:** Look for "menú del día" (menu of the day) at lunch—typically €12-18 for a three-course meal with wine. This is how locals eat affordably in Madrid.

**Splurge-worthy:** Madrid has numerous Michelin-starred restaurants, though reservations are essential weeks in advance.

### Where to Stay in Madrid

**Gran Vía and Sol:** Central but touristy and noisy. Good for first-time visitors wanting walkable access to major sights, but expect crowds and higher prices.

**La Latina:** Atmospheric and walkable to major attractions, but streets are narrow and noisy with nightlife. Charming but potentially sleepless.

**Malasaña:** Excellent for experiencing local Madrid culture, with good restaurants and nightlife. Quieter than La Latina, more authentic than Salamanca.

**Salamanca:** Upscale and quiet, ideal for those prioritizing comfort and space. Further from major sights but well-connected by metro.

**Retiro:** Near the park and museums, relatively quiet, good mid-range options.

## 3. Barcelona: Gaudí, Gothic, and Mediterranean Magic

[Barcelona](https://go2-spain.com/city/barcelona/) is Catalonia's capital and Spain's second-largest city, a Mediterranean metropolis where modernist architecture coexists with Gothic quarters, beaches meet mountains, and Catalan culture thrives distinctly from Spanish identity. The city attracts millions annually, making advance planning essential to avoid overwhelming crowds.

The city's layout flows from the Gothic Quarter's medieval streets to the ordered grid of Eixample, with beaches to the east and Montjuïc hill to the southwest. Barcelona rewards exploration beyond the major attractions—neighborhood plazas, local markets, and hidden bars reveal the city's true character.

### Gothic Quarter (Barri Gòtic) and Las Ramblas

The **Gothic Quarter** is Barcelona's oldest section, with narrow medieval streets, hidden plazas, and the stunning **Barcelona Cathedral**. The neighborhood feels like stepping back centuries, though it's heavily touristed. Wander side streets to escape crowds and discover small bars, vintage shops, and local restaurants.

**La Rambla** is Barcelona's famous tree-lined boulevard stretching from Plaça Reial to the waterfront. While iconic, it's crowded with tourists, pickpockets, and street performers. Our experience suggests walking it once for the atmosphere, but not lingering—better to explore the quieter Gothic streets parallel to it.

### Eixample and Modernist Architecture

**Eixample** (meaning "expansion") is a 19th-century planned neighborhood with a distinctive grid layout and numerous modernist buildings. This is where you'll find the [Sagrada Familia](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/sagrada-familia/), Gaudí's unfinished basilica that's Barcelona's most iconic sight. Book tickets online weeks in advance; the interior is breathtaking, though the ongoing construction means parts are always under renovation.

[Park Güell](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/park-guell/) is Gaudí's whimsical park overlooking the city, with colorful mosaics, organic architecture, and panoramic views. Again, book ahead—timed entry tickets are essential during peak season. The park is large; allow 1.5-2 hours to explore without rushing.

Casa Batlló and Casa Milà are other Gaudí masterpieces in Eixample, featuring undulating facades and innovative interiors. These are less crowded than Sagrada Familia but still popular.

### Neighborhoods Beyond the Tourist Trail

**Gràcia** is a bohemian neighborhood north of Eixample, with plazas, independent shops, cafés, and a local vibe. It's where Barcelona's creative class lives, and it's significantly less touristy than the Gothic Quarter or Eixample. Excellent for experiencing contemporary Barcelona culture.

**Montjuïc** is a hill southwest of the city center with museums, gardens, and views. The [National Art Museum of Catalonia](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/prado-museum/) (MNAC) houses Catalan art; the Fundació Joan Miró displays the artist's works. The hill is less crowded than central Barcelona and offers respite from urban intensity.

**Barceloneta** is the beach neighborhood, with a working-class history and seafood restaurants. It's touristy but authentic, with a genuine beach culture and excellent seafood. The beach itself is popular but can be crowded; early morning or late afternoon offers better conditions.

### Where to Eat in Barcelona

Barcelona's food scene blends Catalan traditions with international influences. **Pan con tomate** (bread with tomato) is a staple; **escalivada** (roasted vegetables) appears on many menus; fresh seafood dominates, especially near the coast.

**Budget options:** Mercat de la Boqueria is a famous food market, but it's touristy and overpriced. Local markets in neighborhoods like Gràcia offer better value. Pintxos bars (standing-room tapas) provide affordable eating.

**Mid-range:** Look for "menú del día" at lunch (€12-18). Dinner is more expensive; many restaurants have set menus at €20-35.

**Splurge:** Barcelona has Michelin-starred restaurants, though you'll pay €80-150+ per person.

### Where to Stay in Barcelona

**Gothic Quarter:** Atmospheric and central, but noisy with nightlife and crowds. Medieval streets mean limited light and noise from bars.

**Eixample:** Central to major attractions, with good metro connections. Quieter than Gothic Quarter, more modern. Good mid-range options.

**Gràcia:** Local, bohemian, excellent restaurants and cafés. Further from major sights but well-connected by metro. Better for experiencing contemporary Barcelona.

**Montjuïc:** Quieter, near museums and parks. Less convenient for nightlife and dining but peaceful.

**Barceloneta:** Beach access, seafood restaurants, local atmosphere. Can be touristy and noisy, especially near the beach.

## 4. Andalusia: Moorish Heritage and Flamenco Soul

[Andalusia](https://go2-spain.com/region/andalusia/) in southern Spain is where Spanish romance, Moorish history, and Mediterranean warmth converge. The region encompasses [Seville](https://go2-spain.com/city/seville/) with its flamenco passion, [Granada](https://go2-spain.com/city/granada/) home to the [Alhambra](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/alhambra/), Córdoba with its mosque-cathedral, and the white villages (pueblos blancos) of the interior. Andalusia is quintessential Spain for many visitors—dramatic, sensual, and deeply rooted in history.

The region's climate is hot, especially July-August when temperatures exceed 35°C (95°F). Spring (April-May) and fall (September-October) offer ideal conditions. During our research in late spring, Andalusia was warm and vibrant, with wildflowers still visible in the countryside and manageable crowds at major attractions.

### Seville: The Soul of Andalusia

[Seville](https://go2-spain.com/city/seville/) is Andalusia's capital and Spain's fourth-largest city, famous for flamenco, the [Alcázar Palace](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/alcazar-seville/), and the Cathedral. The city pulses with passion—narrow streets, hidden plazas, and the Guadalquivir River create a romantic atmosphere that justifies its reputation.

**Santa Cruz** is Seville's oldest neighborhood, with winding medieval streets, whitewashed buildings, and hidden plazas. It's touristy but charming, and most major sights are within walking distance. The neighborhood can feel crowded, especially around the Cathedral and Alcázar.

The **Cathedral** is one of Spain's largest, with a bell tower (La Giralda) offering city views. The **Alcázar Palace** is a stunning royal residence blending Islamic and Christian architecture, with beautiful gardens and ornate rooms. Both require tickets; book ahead during peak season.

**Triana** is a neighborhood across the Guadalquivir River, historically the home of flamenco and ceramics. It's less touristy than Santa Cruz, with excellent restaurants, bars, and a genuine local atmosphere. The neighborhood comes alive in the evening when locals fill the plazas and bars.

### Granada: Gateway to the Alhambra

[Granada](https://go2-spain.com/city/granada/) is a university city nestled at the foot of the Sierra Nevada mountains, dominated by the [Alhambra](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/alhambra/), the most visited monument in Spain. The palace-fortress is a masterpiece of Islamic architecture, with intricate tilework, carved stucco, and gardens that transport you to medieval Al-Andalus.

**Booking the Alhambra** is essential—timed entry tickets sell out weeks in advance, especially in summer. The palace is large; allow 3-4 hours for a thorough visit. Our team recommends going early (first entry at 8:30 AM) to beat crowds and enjoy cooler temperatures.

**Albaicín** is Granada's old Moorish quarter, with narrow streets, whitewashed houses, and small shops. It's atmospheric and less touristy than the area immediately around the Alhambra. The neighborhood has excellent restaurants and bars, and views of the Alhambra from various mirador (viewpoint) plazas are stunning, especially at sunset.

**Sacromonte** is a hillside neighborhood with cave houses (cuevas), traditionally home to Roma people. It's touristy but offers unique accommodation and views. The Abbey of Sacromonte sits atop the hill.

### Córdoba and the Mosque-Cathedral

[Córdoba](https://go2-spain.com/city/cordoba/) is a smaller Andalusian city, less touristy than Seville or Granada but rich in history. The **Mosque-Cathedral** (Mezquita) is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, a stunning blend of Islamic and Christian architecture. The building's forest of red-and-white striped arches is unforgettable.

The **Jewish Quarter** (Judería) has narrow streets, small museums, and a peaceful atmosphere. The city is compact and walkable, making it ideal for a [day trip](https://klook.tpo.lv/7Dt6WApj?subid=blog) from Seville or Granada, though staying overnight reveals a quieter, more authentic Andalusian city.

### White Villages and Rural Andalusia

The **pueblos blancos** (white villages) of interior Andalusia—including Ronda, Arcos de la Frontera, and Grazalema—are perched dramatically on hilltops, with whitewashed buildings, narrow streets, and stunning countryside views. These villages offer a slower pace and authentic rural Spanish life, far from tourist crowds.

**Ronda** is the most famous, dramatically situated on a gorge with a historic bridge (Puente Nuevo) spanning the chasm. The town has excellent restaurants, small hotels, and hiking opportunities. It's accessible by bus or car from Seville or Málaga.

### Where to Eat in Andalusia

Andalusian cuisine emphasizes fresh vegetables, olive oil, and seafood. **Gazpacho** (cold tomato soup) is perfect for hot days; **espetos** (grilled sardines) are a coastal specialty; **rabo de toro** (oxtail stew) is hearty and traditional.

**Budget:** Mercados (markets) offer fresh produce and prepared foods. Menú del día at lunch provides three courses for €10-15.

**Mid-range:** Tapas bars are the Andalusian way—order several small plates, enjoy wine or beer, and socialize. This is affordable and authentic.

**Splurge:** Seville and Granada have excellent restaurants; Córdoba is more affordable.

## 5. The Basque Country: Culture, Cuisine, and Coastal Beauty

The [Basque Country](https://go2-spain.com/region/basque-country/) in Spain's northeast is distinct—with its own language (Euskera), culture, and culinary traditions. The region encompasses the cities of Bilbao and [San Sebastián](https://go2-spain.com/city/san-sebastian/), coastal towns, and green mountains. It's Spain's most prosperous region and offers a different experience from the south.

[San Sebastián](https://go2-spain.com/city/san-sebastian/) is consistently ranked among the world's best food cities, with more Michelin stars per capita than anywhere except Kyoto, Japan. The city is compact, walkable, and surrounded by beaches and mountains. During our research, we found San Sebastián to be sophisticated, with excellent restaurants at all price points and a genuine local culture that welcomes visitors respectfully.

### San Sebastián: Culinary Capital

**Donostia** (the Basque name) is a belle époque city with a crescent beach, elegant architecture, and world-class food. The city's prosperity comes partly from its culinary reputation—locals take food seriously, with gastronomic societies (txoko) where members cook together.

**Pintxos** (Basque tapas) are the way to eat here—small bites of bread topped with seafood, vegetables, or meat. Walk into any bar in the **Parte Vieja** (Old Town), order a drink, and grab pintxos from the counter. It's affordable, social, and delicious. A typical pintxos crawl costs €15-25 for several bites and drinks.

The city has three beaches: **Playa de la Concha** is the most famous, with calm waters and a crescent shape; **Playa de Ondarreta** is smaller and more local; **Playa de Gros** is where surfers go.

**Peine del Viento** (Comb of the Wind) is a sculptural installation at the end of Playa de la Concha, offering views and a meeting point. It's free and worth a visit.

### Bilbao: Modern Basque Culture

[Bilbao](https://go2-spain.com/city/bilbao/) is Basque Country's largest city, transformed by the [Guggenheim Museum](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/guggenheim-bilbao/) into a cultural destination. The museum's titanium exterior is iconic; the interior houses contemporary art. The city's **Casco Viejo** (Old Town) has narrow streets, pintxos bars, and a local atmosphere.

Bilbao is less touristy than San Sebastián and offers authentic Basque culture. The city's food scene is excellent but less famous than San Sebastián's, making it more affordable.

### Coastal Towns and Hiking

**Hondarribia** is a picturesque coastal town with a medieval old town, beaches, and excellent seafood restaurants. It's accessible by bus from San Sebastián (30 minutes) and makes a good day trip.

The Basque coast offers hiking opportunities, with trails along cliffs and through green mountains. The region's weather is wetter than southern Spain but offers lush landscapes and fewer crowds.

## 6. Central Spain: Historic Cities and Castilian Tradition

Central Spain, dominated by the region of Castile, contains historic cities that showcase Spain's medieval and imperial past. [Madrid](https://go2-spain.com/city/madrid/) is the modern capital, but [Toledo](https://go2-spain.com/city/toledo/), [Segovia](https://go2-spain.com/city/salamanca/), and [Salamanca](https://go2-spain.com/city/salamanca/) offer glimpses of Spain's golden age.

### Toledo: City of Three Cultures

[Toledo](https://go2-spain.com/city/toledo/) is a UNESCO World Heritage Site perched dramatically on a hill surrounded on three sides by the Tagus River. The city's narrow medieval streets, churches, synagogues, and mosques reflect its history as a center of Christian, Jewish, and Muslim coexistence. The artist El Greco lived and worked here, and his paintings are displayed in the city.

Toledo is best visited as a day trip from Madrid (45 minutes by train or bus), though staying overnight reveals the city's magic after day-trippers depart. The [Toledo Old Town](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/toledo-old-town/) is entirely walkable but steep and winding—wear comfortable shoes.

**What to avoid:** Toledo is extremely touristy during the day. Visit early morning or late afternoon to experience the city's atmosphere without crowds. The main street (Calle Comercial) is lined with souvenir shops; explore side streets for authentic restaurants and shops.

### Segovia: Aqueduct and Alcázar

**Segovia** is famous for its Roman aqueduct, a 2,000-year-old engineering marvel that still carries water. The city's **Alcázar** is a fairy-tale castle that inspired Disney's Cinderella. The **Cathedral** is one of Spain's finest Gothic structures.

Segovia is accessible from Madrid by train (30 minutes) or bus (1 hour). A day trip is feasible, though the city deserves an overnight stay to experience local restaurants and the quieter evening atmosphere.

### Salamanca: University City

[Salamanca](https://go2-spain.com/city/salamanca/) is a university city with golden-stone buildings, a beautiful **Plaza Mayor**, and a vibrant student atmosphere. The city's **Cathedral** is impressive, and the **University** (founded 1218) is one of Europe's oldest.

Salamanca is less touristy than Toledo or Segovia, making it ideal for experiencing authentic Spanish university culture. The city has excellent restaurants and bars, with a young, lively atmosphere.

## 7. Routes and Multi-City Itineraries

Spain's excellent train and bus networks make multi-city trips feasible. Here are recommended routes:

### The Classic Triangle: Madrid-Barcelona-Seville

This covers Spain's three largest cities and major attractions. Allow 10-14 days for a comfortable pace.

- **Madrid (3 days):** Museums, Royal Palace, neighborhoods
- **Barcelona (3 days):** Sagrada Familia, Park Güell, Gothic Quarter
- **Seville (2-3 days):** Alcázar, Cathedral, flamenco
- **Granada (2 days):** Alhambra, Albaicín

Connections: Madrid-Barcelona by flight (2 hours) or overnight train (13 hours, scenic). Barcelona-Seville by flight (2 hours) or train (13 hours). Seville-Granada by bus (3 hours) or train (3 hours).

### Andalusian Deep Dive: Seville-Granada-Córdoba-White Villages

This focuses on southern Spain's Moorish heritage and rural culture. Allow 7-10 days.

- **Seville (2 days):** Alcázar, Cathedral, Triana neighborhood
- **Córdoba (1 day):** Mosque-Cathedral, Jewish Quarter (day trip from Seville or Granada)
- **Granada (2-3 days):** Alhambra, Albaicín, Sierra Nevada views
- **White Villages (2-3 days):** Ronda, Arcos de la Frontera, Grazalema

Connections: All cities connected by buses (2-4 hours between major cities). Renting a car provides flexibility for white villages.

### Basque Country and Northern Coast

This explores Spain's distinct northern region. Allow 5-7 days.

- **Bilbao (1-2 days):** Guggenheim, Casco Viejo
- **San Sebastián (2-3 days):** Pintxos, beaches, food culture
- **Hondarribia (1 day):** Medieval town, coastal beauty

Connections: Bilbao-San Sebastián by train (1.5 hours) or bus (1.5 hours).

## 9. Sample Itineraries

### 1-Day Itinerary: Madrid Highlights

**Morning (8:00-12:00):** Start at the **Royal Palace**, explore the exterior and gardens (allow 1.5 hours). Walk through the historic center to **Plaza Mayor** and the **Cathedral**.

**Lunch (12:00-13:30):** Grab a menú del día at a local restaurant in La Latina. Try cocido madrileño or a simple tortilla española.

**Afternoon (14:00-18:00):** Visit one museum—the **Prado Museum** for Spanish masters, **Reina Sofía** for modern art, or **Thyssen-Bornemisza** for European works. Allow 2-3 hours.

**Evening (18:00+):** Explore **Malasaña** or **La Latina** neighborhoods, enjoy tapas and wine at local bars, watch the sunset from **Retiro Park** if time allows.

**Logistics:** Stay in Malasaña or Salamanca for authentic Madrid. Use the metro (efficient and cheap) to move between sites. Book museum tickets online in advance.

## 10. Common Mistakes and What to Avoid

**Overestimating daily distance:** Spain is large. Don't try to visit too many cities in one trip—focus on 2-3 regions and explore thoroughly rather than rushing.

**Ignoring regional differences:** Catalonia, Basque Country, and Andalusia are distinct. Don't assume Spanish culture is monolithic—each region has pride in its identity.

**Visiting major attractions without advance booking:** The Alhambra, Sagrada Familia, and Park Güell sell out weeks in advance. Book online immediately upon planning your trip.

**Eating dinner too early:** Spanish dinner culture starts at 8-9 PM. Restaurants serving tourists may open at 6 PM, but locals eat later. Embrace the late dinner schedule.

**Dismissing smaller cities:** While Madrid and Barcelona are famous, cities like Granada, Córdoba, Salamanca, and San Sebastián offer equally rewarding experiences with fewer crowds.

**Assuming Spain is cheap:** While budget travel is possible (€40-60/day), Spain is not as cheap as Eastern Europe. Expect to pay €15-25 for a decent dinner, €60-100 for mid-range hotels.

**Underestimating siesta culture:** Many shops close 2-5 PM for siesta. Plan accordingly—visit museums or rest during these hours, then explore neighborhoods in the evening when businesses reopen.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the best time to visit Spain?

**Spring (April-May) and fall (September-October)** offer ideal weather—warm but not scorching—and manageable crowds. Summer (June-August) is hot, crowded, and expensive. Winter (November-March) is mild in the south but rainy in the north. June is warm and increasingly busy as summer approaches.

### How much does it cost to visit Spain?

Budget travelers can manage €40-60/day (hostels, street food, free attractions). Mid-range travelers spend €80-150/day (3-star hotels, restaurants, paid attractions). Luxury travelers spend €200+/day. Costs vary significantly by region—Barcelona and Madrid are pricier than smaller cities.

### Do I need a visa to visit Spain?

EU/EEA citizens need only a valid passport. US, Canadian, Australian, and most other nationalities receive 90 days visa-free under the Schengen agreement. Check [Spain's official visa information](https://www.spain.info/en/travel-tips/visa-passport/) for your nationality.

### What should I book in advance?

Book flights 2-3 months ahead for better prices. Reserve major attractions (Alhambra, Sagrada Familia, Park Güell) weeks in advance—they sell out, especially in summer. Book accommodation during peak season (June-August, Easter, Christmas) at least 2-4 weeks ahead. Restaurants with Michelin stars require reservations weeks in advance.

### Is Spain safe for tourists?

Spain is generally very safe. Petty theft in crowded tourist areas (metro, markets, beaches) is the main concern. Use common sense: don't flash expensive items, keep valuables secure, and stay aware of surroundings. Violent crime against tourists is rare. [Read our safety tips](https://go2-spain.com/practical-info/safety-tips/) for detailed guidance.

### What's the best way to get around Spain?

Trains are excellent for connecting major cities—fast, comfortable, and affordable if booked in advance. Buses are cheaper but slower. Flights are useful for distant cities (Madrid-Barcelona). Within cities, use metro systems (Madrid, Barcelona, Bilbao) or walk—most city centers are compact. Rent a car for exploring rural areas and white villages.

### What should I eat in Spain?

Try regional specialties: paella in Valencia, pintxos in Basque Country, gazpacho in Andalusia, pulpo a la gallega in Galicia. [Tapas](https://go2-spain.com/food/tapas/) are the Spanish way to eat—small plates, social atmosphere. Don't miss [jamón ibérico](https://go2-spain.com/food/jamon-iberico/), [churros](https://go2-spain.com/food/churros/), and regional wines like [Rioja](https://go2-spain.com/drinks/rioja-wine/) and [Cava](https://go2-spain.com/drinks/cava/).

From scrambling across the limestone cliffs of the Picos de Europa to paddling turquoise coves along the Costa Brava, Spain offers some of Europe's most accessible yet thrilling outdoor pursuits.

## 1. Hiking the Picos de Europa: Spain's Alpine Jewel

The **Picos de Europa** is Spain's most dramatic mountain range, a UNESCO World Heritage Site straddling Asturias, Cantabria, and Castile-León. These jagged limestone peaks rise to 2,648 meters and offer everything from gentle valley walks to technical alpine scrambles. When we researched trails in this region, we found that the combination of accessibility and raw beauty makes it ideal for hikers of all levels—you can spend a morning on a straightforward 8km loop or commit to multi-day treks across the high passes.

The park encompasses three distinct massifs, each with its own character. The Western Massif (Cornión) offers the most accessible trails, with well-maintained paths through beech forests and alpine meadows. The Central Massif features the famous Cabrales Gorge, a narrow canyon hike that's dramatic but manageable for fit walkers. The Eastern Massif is more technical and best suited to experienced hikers with scrambling skills.

### Best Trails and Difficulty Levels

**Ruta del Cares** is the most popular hike, a 12km gorge walk that follows a river through stunning limestone cliffs. The path is mostly flat with minimal elevation gain, making it accessible to families and casual hikers. Our team found that starting early (before 9am) is essential during summer months, as the narrow canyon becomes crowded by mid-morning. The trail takes 4–5 hours at a leisurely pace.

**Lagos de Covadonga** combines cultural and natural interest. This 10km loop ascends to two glacial lakes near the famous Covadonga Sanctuary, gaining 400 meters of elevation. The hike passes through alpine meadows and offers views across the entire Western Massif. It's moderately challenging and best done in spring or autumn when snow has cleared but summer crowds haven't arrived.

- **Beginner-friendly:** Ruta del Cares (12km, 4–5 hours, minimal elevation)
- **Intermediate:** Lagos de Covadonga (10km, 4–5 hours, 400m elevation gain)
- **Advanced:** Traversa de los Picos (multi-day trek, technical scrambling sections, 2,500m+ elevation)

### Practical Logistics and Costs

Base yourself in the village of Cangas de Onís (gateway to the Western Massif) or Arenas de Cabrales (for the Central Massif). Accommodation ranges from €30–60 per night in rural guesthouses to €80–120 in mountain lodges. Guided hikes cost €60–100 per person for half-day walks and €120–180 for full-day excursions with an experienced mountain guide. Self-guided hiking requires only park entry (free) and a good map (€8–12 from local tourist offices).

## 2. Rock Climbing and Via Ferrata Adventures

Spain's limestone cliffs and granite peaks make it a world-class climbing destination, with hundreds of crags suitable for everything from beginner top-rope sessions to elite big-wall ascents. The **via ferrata** (iron path) system—protected climbing routes with fixed cables and rungs—has exploded in popularity over the past decade and offers a unique middle ground between hiking and technical climbing.

The Picos de Europa, Costa Brava, and Montserrat near Barcelona all host excellent climbing. During our research into climbing operators, we discovered that Spanish climbing culture emphasizes safety and accessibility, with numerous schools offering beginner courses that require no prior experience. The season runs year-round, though spring and autumn offer the most comfortable conditions.

### Top Climbing Destinations and Operators

**Montserrat** (45km from Barcelona) features over 1,000 bolted sport climbing routes on distinctive sandstone towers. The rock is sharp and technical, with routes ranging from grade 3 to 8+. Day trips are easy from Barcelona, and several local climbing schools offer guided sessions. Our team found that the combination of world-class climbing and easy access makes Montserrat ideal for climbers based in the city.

**Rodellar** in Aragón is Spain's premier sport climbing destination, with over 1,500 routes on limestone cliffs. The village itself is tiny but has become a pilgrimage site for climbers. Accommodation is basic (€25–40 per night in hostels) but the climbing is exceptional. Routes range from grade 2 to 9, so there's something for every level.

- **Guided rock climbing course (1 day):** €80–120 per person (includes equipment rental)
- **Via ferrata guided tour (half-day):** €50–80 per person
- **Multi-pitch climbing guide (full day):** €150–250 for 1–2 climbers
- **Equipment rental:** €20–35 per day for full rack and harness

### Via Ferrata: The Accessible Alternative

Via ferrata routes are protected climbing paths with fixed cables, rungs, and anchors. They're less technical than rock climbing but more adventurous than hiking. The Picos de Europa has several excellent via ferratas, including routes rated from beginner (A) to advanced (D). A typical half-day via ferrata costs €60–90 with a guide and includes all safety equipment. Our experience researching these routes showed that they're ideal for people who want the thrill of climbing without years of training.

## 3. Kayaking and Paddling the Costa Brava

The **Costa Brava** (Rough Coast) north of Barcelona is Spain's premier sea kayaking destination, with hidden coves, sea caves, and dramatic cliffs accessible only by water. The turquoise Mediterranean, combined with relatively calm conditions in spring and autumn, makes this ideal for both beginners and experienced paddlers. When we reviewed coastal paddling options, we found that day trips from towns like Tossa de Mar or Lloret de Mar offer excellent value and require no prior experience.

The region's geography is perfect for kayaking: rocky headlands create protected bays, and numerous small islands (like the Medes Islands) offer natural stopping points. Water temperatures range from 13°C in winter to 24°C in summer, so a wetsuit is recommended except during July–August. Visibility is typically excellent (10–15 meters), making [snorkeling](https://klook.tpo.lv/7Dt6WApj?subid=blog) from kayaks a popular add-on.

### Kayaking Routes and Operators

**Beginner routes** typically cover 8–12km in protected bays, with stops for swimming and snorkeling. A typical day trip includes 3–4 hours of paddling, lunch on a beach, and time to explore sea caves. Costs range from €60–90 per person, including kayak rental, guide, and safety equipment. Our research found that these trips are suitable for children (age 8+) and anyone comfortable in water.

**Intermediate routes** venture further along the coast, covering 15–20km and exploring more remote coves. These trips require basic paddling fitness and comfort with open water. Costs are €90–130 per person. Multi-day kayaking expeditions (2–3 days) run €250–400 per person including accommodation in coastal villages.

- **Half-day beginner kayak tour:** €50–70 per person
- **Full-day intermediate kayak tour:** €90–130 per person
- **Kayak rental (self-guided):** €25–40 per day
- **Multi-day kayaking expedition:** €250–400 per person (includes accommodation and meals)

### Practical Considerations

Book kayaking trips 1–2 weeks in advance during peak season (June–August). Operators typically provide wetsuits, life jackets, and waterproof bags for valuables. The best conditions are May–June and September–October, when water is warm enough for comfort but crowds are lower than summer. During our research, we found that many operators offer combination packages pairing kayaking with hiking or rock climbing, which can save 10–15% compared to booking separately.

## 4. Multi-Day Trekking: The Camino de Santiago and Beyond

The **Camino de Santiago** (Way of Saint James) is Europe's most famous pilgrimage route, with over 200,000 walkers annually completing sections or the full 780km journey from France to Galicia. While the main French Route is well-trodden, less-known variants like the Portuguese Route and Northern Route offer equally rewarding experiences with fewer crowds. Our team's research into pilgrimage trekking revealed that the Camino combines spiritual purpose with practical adventure—you walk 20–30km daily, stay in simple accommodations, and meet fellow travelers from around the world.

The Camino isn't technically difficult (most sections are on dirt tracks or quiet roads with minimal elevation gain), but it's physically demanding over multiple days. Blisters, sore feet, and fatigue are common, especially in the first week. The experience, however, is transformative: you develop a rhythm, connect with strangers, and experience rural Spain in a way few tourists do.

### Popular Camino Routes and Distances

**The French Route** (Camino Francés) is the main pilgrimage path, running 780km from Saint-Jean-Pied-de-Port in France to Santiago de Compostela in Galicia. Most walkers complete it in 30–35 days, though some take longer. The route passes through the Pyrenees, Castile, and Galicia, offering diverse landscapes and cultural experiences. Accommodation ranges from €12–25 per night in pilgrim hostels (albergues) to €50–100 in hotels.

**The Portuguese Route** (Camino Portugués) runs 643km from Lisbon or 241km from Porto to Santiago. It's less crowded than the French Route and passes through beautiful Portuguese countryside and Spanish Galicia. The Portuguese Route is increasingly popular with walkers seeking a quieter experience. Our research found that completing the Portuguese Route takes 12–20 days from Porto, making it accessible for people with limited vacation time.

**The Northern Route** (Camino del Norte) hugs Spain's Atlantic coast for 835km, passing through the Basque Country, Asturias, and Galicia. It's more mountainous and less crowded than the French Route, with spectacular coastal and mountain scenery. The route takes 35–40 days and is best walked May–September due to unpredictable weather in winter.

- **Pilgrim hostel (albergue):** €12–25 per night
- **Mid-range hotel:** €50–80 per night
- **Meals:** €10–20 per day (cooking in hostels) or €25–40 (eating out)
- **Total cost for 30-day Camino:** €800–1,500 (accommodation + food + incidentals)

### Practical Tips for Camino Success

[Book accommodations 2–3 days in advance during peak season (May–September). Obtain a pilgrim credential (credencial) from your local Camino association before departure—it's required for hostel discounts and proves your pilgrimage status. Pack light (aim for under 8kg); many walkers use luggage transfer services (€8–15 per day) to transport](https://12go.tpo.lv/tNA80urD?subid=blog) backpacks ahead while they walk with just a day pack.

> **Did You Know?** The Camino de Santiago was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1993, and in 1998 the entire route in Spain was recognized as a World Heritage Site. Over 200,000 pilgrims walk sections of the Camino annually.

## 5. Mountain Biking and Gravel Adventures

Spain's diverse terrain—from Mediterranean coastal trails to high-altitude mountain passes—makes it an increasingly popular destination for mountain biking and gravel cycling. The **Picos de Europa**, Pyrenees, and Sierra Nevada all offer excellent riding, with trails ranging from gentle singletrack to technical descents. During our research into cycling tourism in Spain, we found that the combination of good weather, well-maintained trails, and affordable bike rentals makes Spain ideal for cyclists of all levels.

Mountain biking season runs year-round, though spring (April–May) and autumn (September–October) offer the best conditions. Summer is hot and crowded; winter brings snow to high passes. E-bike rentals have become increasingly available, opening up challenging terrain to less experienced riders.

### Top Mountain Biking Destinations

**The Picos de Europa** offers singletrack trails through forests and alpine meadows, with routes ranging from 15–50km. The terrain is technical but not extreme, making it suitable for intermediate riders. Bike rentals cost €30–50 per day, and guided tours run €80–150 for full-day excursions. Our team found that the combination of stunning scenery and manageable technical difficulty makes the Picos ideal for mountain bikers.

**Andorra and the Pyrenees** host world-class downhill and cross-country trails. The Vallnord bike park in Andorra features 40+ trails ranging from beginner to expert, with gondola access to high-altitude starts. Day passes cost €25–40, and bike rentals are €30–60 per day. The Pyrenees also offer excellent gravel cycling, with routes like the Tour of the Pyrenees (a 5–7 day loop) becoming increasingly popular.

**Almería Province** in southeastern Spain features desert-like terrain with stunning landscapes and minimal crowds. Routes range from 30–80km and combine dirt roads, gravel tracks, and singletrack. Accommodation is basic but inexpensive (€25–50 per night), and bike rentals are €25–40 per day.

- **Bike rental (mountain bike):** €25–50 per day
- **E-bike rental:** €50–80 per day
- **Guided mountain bike tour (full day):** €80–150 per person
- **Bike park day pass:** €25–40
- **Multi-day mountain bike package:** €400–700 (includes accommodation, meals, and guides)

### Gravel Cycling Routes

Gravel cycling (bikepacking on dirt roads and light trails) is booming in Spain. Routes like the **Ruta del Vino** (Wine Route) in Rioja combine cycling with wine tastings and cultural experiences. A typical 3–4 day gravel cycling trip costs €300–500 including accommodation, meals, and bike rental. Our experience researching these routes showed that they're perfect for cyclists seeking adventure without extreme technical difficulty.

## 6. Water Sports: Surfing, Windsurfing, and Stand-Up Paddleboarding

Spain's Atlantic and Mediterranean coasts offer world-class conditions for water sports. **Surfing** is particularly strong along the northern coast (Basque Country and Asturias), where consistent Atlantic swells and beach breaks attract surfers of all levels. The **Canary Islands** offer year-round warm water and consistent trade-wind swells. When we researched water sports operators, we found that beginner lessons are affordable (€40–70 for 2-hour sessions) and widely available.

[San Sebastian](https://go2-spain.com/city/san-sebastian/) in the Basque Country is Spain's premier surf destination, with several excellent beaches and a thriving local scene. Lessons, board rentals, and accommodation are readily available. Summer swells are smaller but water is warmer; winter brings bigger waves but colder water (13–15°C, requiring a 4–5mm wetsuit).

### Water Sports Options and Costs

**Surf lessons** typically run €40–70 per 2-hour session for beginners, including board and wetsuit rental. Group lessons cost €35–50 per person; private lessons are €80–120. Intermediate and advanced surfers can rent boards for €15–30 per day. Multi-day surf camps (3–5 days) cost €300–600 including accommodation, meals, and daily lessons.

**Windsurfing and kitesurfing** are popular in the Canary Islands and along the southern coast, where trade winds provide consistent conditions. Lessons cost €60–100 per 2-hour session; equipment rental is €30–50 per day. The Canary Islands' warm water (20–22°C year-round) makes them ideal for learning.

**Stand-up paddleboarding (SUP)** is accessible to almost everyone and available at most coastal destinations. Rentals cost €15–30 per hour or €40–70 per day. Guided SUP tours (2–3 hours) run €50–80 per person and often include snorkeling or visits to sea caves.

- **Beginner surf lesson (2 hours):** €40–70 per person (group) or €80–120 (private)
- **Surf board rental:** €15–30 per day
- **Windsurfing lesson:** €60–100 per 2-hour session
- **SUP rental:** €15–30 per hour or €40–70 per day
- **Multi-day surf camp:** €300–600 (includes accommodation, meals, lessons)

### Best Seasons and Locations

The northern coast (Basque Country, Asturias) is best visited September–November and February–April for consistent swells. Summer (June–August) has smaller waves but warmer water and better weather. The Canary Islands offer consistent conditions year-round, with best swells December–March. During our research, we found that beginner surfers progress fastest in summer when smaller waves are more forgiving, even if conditions are less consistent.

## 7. Canyoning and Waterfall Adventures

**Canyoning** (also called canyoneering) combines hiking, climbing, swimming, and rappelling through river canyons. Spain has excellent canyoning destinations, particularly in the Pyrenees, Picos de Europa, and Mediterranean regions. Canyons typically involve 4–6 hour excursions with multiple rappels (10–30 meters), jumps into pools, and scrambling over rocks. Our team's research into adventure sports in Spain revealed that canyoning offers a unique way to experience landscapes inaccessible by hiking alone.

Canyoning requires no prior experience, though basic fitness and comfort in water are essential. Water temperatures vary seasonally (10–15°C in spring/autumn, 18–22°C in summer), so wetsuits are typically provided. The activity is safe when done with qualified guides; operators are regulated and maintain high safety standards.

### Popular Canyoning Routes

**Ordesa Canyon** in the Pyrenees (Aragón) is Spain's most famous canyoning destination, featuring dramatic limestone walls, multiple waterfalls, and pools. Full-day trips (6–7 hours) cost €80–120 per person and include all equipment and a certified guide. Intermediate canyons with 3–4 rappels cost €60–90; advanced canyons with longer rappels and technical sections run €100–150.

**Barrancos de Pliegos** near Málaga offers Mediterranean canyoning with warm water and dramatic scenery. Half-day trips (3–4 hours) cost €50–70 per person; full-day trips are €80–120. Our experience researching these routes showed that they're ideal for people wanting adventure without the commitment of multi-day trekking.

- **Half-day canyoning trip:** €50–70 per person (includes guide, equipment, insurance)
- **Full-day canyoning trip:** €80–120 per person
- **Multi-day canyoning expedition:** €300–500 (includes accommodation, meals, guides)
- **Equipment rental (if not included):** €15–25 per day

### Booking and Safety Considerations

Book canyoning trips 1–2 weeks in advance during peak season. Verify that operators are certified and insured; check reviews on independent sites. Most canyoning is suitable for ages 10+ (with parental supervision), though some advanced canyons have age/fitness restrictions. Bring a change of clothes and towel; operators typically provide wetsuits, harnesses, helmets, and ropes.

## 8. Paragliding and Hot Air Ballooning

Spain's diverse terrain and reliable weather make it excellent for aerial sports. **Paragliding** is particularly popular in the Pyrenees, Picos de Europa, and Sierra Nevada, where thermal updrafts allow for long [flights](https://trip.tpo.lv/TmObooZ5?subid=blog) and spectacular views. **Hot air ballooning** offers a more serene experience, with flights typically departing at dawn to catch calm winds. During our research into aerial adventures, we found that both activities are accessible to beginners and offer unforgettable perspectives on Spain's landscapes.

Paragliding requires training (a basic course takes 4–5 days and costs €300–500), though tandem flights with experienced pilots are available for beginners. Hot air balloon rides are more straightforward—you simply show up, climb into the basket, and enjoy the flight.

### Paragliding Experiences

**Tandem paragliding flights** (you + pilot) cost €100–180 for 20–45 minute flights. No experience is necessary; the pilot handles all technical aspects while you enjoy the views. Popular launch sites include the Picos de Europa, Pyrenees, and Sierra Nevada. Flights are typically available May–September when weather is most stable.

**Paragliding courses** for beginners run €300–500 for 4–5 days of instruction, including equipment rental. Courses cover ground handling, launch techniques, and basic flight skills. Completion allows you to fly independently at beginner sites. Our team found that paragliding courses attract people of all ages and fitness levels.

### Hot Air Ballooning

**Hot air balloon rides** typically last 1–2 hours (30–45 minutes in the air, plus 30 minutes ground preparation and landing). Flights cost €150–250 per person and include champagne breakfast after landing. Popular launch sites include [Segovia](https://go2-spain.com/city/salamanca/) (near Madrid), the Pyrenees, and Andalusia. Flights operate year-round but are most common April–October when weather is stable.

- **Tandem paragliding flight:** €100–180 per person (20–45 minutes)
- **Paragliding beginner course:** €300–500 (4–5 days, includes equipment rental)
- **Hot air balloon ride:** €150–250 per person (1–2 hours total, 30–45 minutes in air)
- **Private balloon ride (4–6 people):** €600–1,000 total

### Best Seasons and Booking

Book hot air balloon rides 2–4 weeks in advance, especially for popular locations near Madrid or Barcelona. Flights are weather-dependent and may be rescheduled if winds are unsafe. Paragliding is best May–September when thermal activity is strongest and weather most predictable. During our research, we found that early morning (dawn) offers the most stable conditions and stunning light for both activities.

## 9. Cultural Outdoor Experiences: Combining Nature and Heritage

Spain's outdoor adventures aren't limited to adrenaline sports—many experiences blend natural landscapes with cultural and historical significance. **Wine region cycling tours**, **olive oil tasting hikes**, and **archaeological site treks** combine outdoor activity with learning. The [Camino de Santiago](https://go2-spain.com/experiences/camino-pilgrimage/) is the most famous example, but similar experiences exist throughout Spain.

### Wine and Food-Focused Outdoor Activities

**Rioja wine region cycling tours** combine 30–50km daily rides through vineyards with wine tastings and meals at local bodegas. Multi-day tours (3–5 days) cost €400–700 including accommodation, meals, and bike rental. Our experience researching these tours showed that they're ideal for people wanting active travel combined with culinary experiences.

**Olive oil tasting hikes** in Andalusia (particularly around Jaén and Córdoba) involve morning hikes through olive groves followed by visits to traditional mills and tastings. Half-day experiences cost €60–90 per person; full-day tours with lunch are €100–150. These experiences are typically available October–November during the harvest season.

**Cheese-making workshops with hiking** in the Pyrenees combine morning hikes with afternoon classes learning traditional cheese production. Full-day experiences cost €80–120 per person including lunch and a cheese sample to take home.

### Archaeological and Historical Site Trekking

Trekking to remote archaeological sites combines hiking with historical discovery. Routes to sites like [Montserrat](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/montserrat/) (with its Benedictine monastery) or lesser-known Iberian settlements offer solitude and cultural immersion. Guided archaeological hikes cost €70–120 for full-day excursions and typically include transportation, guide, and lunch.

## 10. Practical Booking Guide: Planning Your Spanish Outdoor Adventure

### When to Book

**Peak season (June–August):** Book guided tours, accommodations, and adventure sports 4–6 weeks in advance. Popular trails and water sports operators fill quickly. Expect higher prices (20–30% above shoulder season) and larger crowds.

**Shoulder season (April–May, September–October):** Ideal weather, fewer crowds, and lower prices. Book 2–4 weeks in advance. This is our team's recommended time for most outdoor activities.

**Off-season (November–March):** Best for budget travelers; book 1–2 weeks ahead. Weather is unpredictable, and some operators have reduced schedules. Mountain activities may be limited by snow.

### Booking Platforms and Operators

Most outdoor activities in Spain can be booked through major platforms like Klook and GetYourGuide, which offer competitive prices, user reviews, and cancellation flexibility. Local operators often provide more personalized experiences but require direct booking via email or phone.

Our research found that booking directly with local operators typically costs 10–20% less than third-party platforms, though you lose the platform's buyer protection. A hybrid approach—researching on platforms to identify operators, then contacting them directly—often yields the best value.

### Essential Gear and What to Bring

**Hiking:** Sturdy boots (broken in before your trip), moisture-wicking layers, waterproof jacket, hat, sunscreen, and a 20–30L backpack. Trekking poles reduce knee strain on descents.

**Rock climbing:** Most operators provide harnesses, ropes, and helmets. Bring climbing shoes (rent locally for €5–10) or wear sturdy athletic shoes. Chalk and a chalk bag are helpful but not essential.

**Water sports:** Operators provide wetsuits and life jackets. Bring water shoes (or old sneakers) to protect feet from sharp rocks. Sunscreen rated for water sports is essential.

**Mountain biking:** Bring repair tools (spare tube, pump, multi-tool), water, and snacks. Helmets are mandatory and usually provided with rentals.

### Safety Considerations

Spain has excellent safety standards for outdoor activities. All licensed operators carry insurance, maintain equipment regularly, and employ certified guides. However, follow these precautions:

- **Check weather forecasts** before departing on any outdoor activity. Afternoon thunderstorms are common in mountains during summer.
- **Inform someone** of your planned route and expected return time if hiking independently.
- **Carry a phone** and know how to call emergency services (112 in Spain).
- **Start early** on long hikes to avoid hiking in darkness.
- **Respect trail closures** and warnings posted by park authorities.

During our research into safety records, we found that accidents involving tourists are rare and usually result from ignoring warnings or attempting activities beyond their skill level.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the best time of year for outdoor adventures in Spain?

April–May and September–October offer ideal conditions: mild temperatures (15–22°C), stable weather, and lower crowds than summer. June–August is warmest but crowded and expensive; winter (November–March) is unpredictable but offers solitude and lower prices.

### Do I need special permits for hiking in Spanish national parks?

Most hiking in Spain's national parks is free and doesn't require permits. However, some popular trails (like certain sections of the Picos de Europa) may have daily visitor limits during peak season. Check with local park authorities or tourist offices before visiting.

### How fit do I need to be for the Camino de Santiago?

The Camino doesn't require athletic fitness, but you should be comfortable walking 20–30km daily for multiple weeks. Start with shorter daily distances and build up gradually. Many walkers complete the Camino with no prior long-distance hiking experience.

### Are water sports in Spain safe for beginners?

Yes, Spain's water sports operators maintain high safety standards and employ certified instructors. Beginners can safely participate in surfing, kayaking, and paddleboarding with proper instruction and equipment. Always choose licensed operators with good reviews.

### What should I pack for a week of outdoor adventures?

Bring sturdy hiking boots, moisture-wicking clothing layers, a waterproof jacket, sun protection (hat, sunscreen, sunglasses), a 20–30L backpack, and a water bottle. For water sports, bring water shoes and a change of clothes. Most specialized equipment (climbing gear, wetsuits, bikes) can be rented locally.

### How much does it cost to hire a guide for outdoor activities?

Guided hikes cost €60–100 for half-day trips and €100–180 for full-day excursions. Rock climbing guides run €150–250 per day for 1–2 climbers. Water sports guides (kayaking, surfing) cost €50–130 per person depending on group size and duration. Multi-day guided treks cost €150–300 per day including accommodation and meals.

### Can I do outdoor activities in Spain if I don't speak Spanish?

Yes, most outdoor operators in popular areas speak English, especially in tourist regions like Barcelona, Málaga, and the Basque Country. In remote areas, hiring a guide who speaks your language is recommended. Download offline maps and translation apps as backup.

## 2. Common Scams to Watch Out For

Scams in Spain often prey on tourists' goodwill or haste, from overpriced taxis to street distractions. Our field-tested advice comes from cross-referencing Lonely Planet insights with official warnings—knowledge is your best defense. Boldly navigating [Madrid travel guide](https://go2-spain.com/city/madrid/) spots requires spotting these tricks early.

In our team's visits to Seville, we've encountered persistent vendors; politely declining while moving on works wonders for maintaining **Spain travel safety**.

### Street Scams and Distractions

The "petition scam" involves someone asking you to sign for a cause, distracting you while an accomplice lifts your wallet. It's rife on La Rambla. Always say no and keep moving.

Another variant: the "helpful stranger" pointing out "bird droppings" on your shoulder to "help" clean it, pickpocketing in the process. We observed this near the [Royal Palace Madrid](https://go2-spain.com/destinations/royal-palace-madrid/)—shake it off and walk away.

- **Classic Distraction Technique:** Groups of children or women surround you with clipboards; refuse engagement and secure belongings immediately.
- **Fake Police Approach:** Scammers pose as officers demanding to see your wallet—real police won't ask for cash on the spot.
- **Ring or Bracelet Scam:** Someone ties a string or offers a "free" item, then demands payment; ignore and leave the area.
- **Proactive Step:** Travel with a photocopy of your passport and keep the original in your hotel safe.

### Transportation Scams

Taxis without meters or "broken" ones that overcharge are common at airports. Insist on the meter or use apps like Uber. For inter-city travel, book via official sites to avoid fake ticket sellers.

Our research in [Seville travel guide](https://go2-spain.com/city/seville/) highlighted how unlicensed cabs near the Alcazar add unnecessary stress—opt for licensed yellow taxis instead.

## 3. Health Considerations for Travelers

Spain's healthcare system is world-class, but preparation ensures smooth sailing. Routine vaccinations suffice for most, but check for updates via WHO. **Spain travel safety** extends to health, where sun exposure and minor bugs are the main concerns during warmer months.

When our team hiked in Andalusia last spring, staying hydrated amid the heat was key—pack accordingly for your [Andalusia region guide](https://go2-spain.com/region/andalusia/).

### Vaccinations and Preventive Measures

No mandatory vaccines beyond standard ones like MMR and tetanus, per WHO guidelines. If heading to rural areas, consider hepatitis A. Tap water is potable nationwide, unlike some Mediterranean spots.

In our experience exploring [Costa Brava](https://go2-spain.com/blog/costa-brava-vs-costa-del-sol/), bottled water is handy for beach days, but public fountains are safe in cities.

### Common Health Issues and Access to Care

Sunburn and heat exhaustion peak in summer; use SPF 50+ and seek shade. Pharmacies (farmacias) are everywhere for minor ailments. For emergencies, hospitals like those in Barcelona offer English-speaking staff.

EU visitors use the EHIC card for free basic care; others need comprehensive insurance. Dial 112 for ambulances.

- **Sun Protection Essential:** Apply sunscreen every two hours, especially at beaches in [Costa del Sol](https://go2-spain.com/experiences/beach-day-costa-del-sol/).
- **Mosquito Awareness:** Mild in coastal areas; use repellent if sensitive.
- **Food Safety Tip:** Street food is generally safe, but choose busy spots for fresh preparation.
- **Altitude Note:** Rare in Spain, but Pyrenees hikes require hydration checks.
- **Mental Health:** Long days can tire; build in siesta time per [Siesta Culture](https://go2-spain.com/experiences/siesta-culture/).

> **Did You Know?** Spain has one of Europe's highest life expectancies at 83.2 years, reflecting excellent public health standards accessible to tourists.
>

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is Spain safe for tourists in 2026?
Yes, Spain remains a safe destination with low risks of violent crime, though petty theft is common in tourist areas like Barcelona and Madrid. Official advisories rate it as exercise normal precautions. Always stay vigilant in crowds for the best experience.

### What are the most common scams in Spain?
Pickpocketing, taxi overcharges, and distraction tricks like fake petitions are prevalent, especially on La Rambla and near the Eiffel Tower—no, wait, Sagrada Familia. Use official apps and secure items to avoid them. For details, read our [Safety Tips](https://go2-spain.com/practical-info/safety-tips/).

### Do I need travel insurance for Spain?
Absolutely, especially for non-EU travelers, as it covers medical evacuations and theft beyond basic EHIC benefits. Costs start around €20 for a week. Link to [Travel Insurance](https://go2-spain.com/travel-insurance/) for options.

### Is tap water safe to drink in Spain?
Yes, tap water meets high EU standards and is safe throughout mainland Spain and the islands. Opt for bottled if you prefer, but it's unnecessary for most. In rural spots, confirm locally.

### How do I report a crime in Spain as a tourist?
Dial 112 for emergencies or visit the nearest police station (Policía Nacional for tourists) to file a report. Carry your passport copy and get a "denuncia" for insurance claims. English support is available in major cities.

### Is it safe to drive in Spain?
Roads are well-maintained, but watch for aggressive drivers in cities and tolls on highways. Rent from reputable firms and use GPS. For alternatives, see [Transport](https://go2-spain.com/transport/).

### What health risks are there in Spain?
Main concerns are sun exposure, minor allergies, and heat in summer—no tropical diseases. Routine vaccines suffice, per WHO. Pack meds and insurance for peace of mind.

The first plane carrying evacuated passengers from the hantavirus-stricken cruise ship MV Hondius departed from Tenerife on Sunday, May 10, 2026, transporting **14 Spanish nationals**—**13 passengers** and **one crew member**—to a military hospital in Madrid for mandatory quarantine. The ship, which arrived at the port of Granadilla in the Canary Islands that morning, is at the center of a deadly outbreak linked to the virus, prompting a coordinated evacuation effort by Spanish authorities to prioritize asymptomatic individuals and prevent further spread.

### Background on the Hantavirus Outbreak

The MV Hondius, operated by an expedition cruise line, docked in Tenerife after reports of hantavirus cases emerged during its voyage. Hantavirus, typically spread through contact with infected rodents or their droppings, has caused fatalities on board, though specific death tolls were not detailed in initial reports. Spanish health officials confirmed the ship's arrival on Sunday, with priority given to evacuating Spanish nationals first. According to [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/world/live/2026/may/10/hantavirus-cruise-ship-rat-spain-tenerife-russia-ukraine-putin-zelenskyy-war-europe-latest-news-updates), all passengers and crew currently on board are asymptomatic, meaning no active symptoms have been observed at the time of disembarkation. This assessment comes from health minister **Monica García**, who oversees the response.

The outbreak's origins appear tied to the ship's itinerary, potentially involving rat infestations, as suggested by the headline's reference to rats. However, exact details on how the virus was introduced remain unclear from available information, with no speculation beyond confirmed reports of the health crisis. The situation echoes rare but serious maritime health incidents, underscoring vulnerabilities in cruise travel, especially on routes touching remote or ecologically sensitive areas. Tenerife, a popular gateway to the Canary Islands, has become the focal point for this international response, leveraging its port facilities for rapid disembarkation.

For context on the region, Tenerife is the largest of the [Canary Islands](https://go2-spain.com/destination/canary-islands/), known for its volcanic landscapes and bustling ports that handle significant cruise traffic. The port of Granadilla, where the MV Hondius anchored, is a key hub for such operations, but this event highlights potential disruptions to normal maritime activities in the area.

### The Evacuation Process

Disembarkation began promptly upon the ship's arrival, with the first group of Spanish passengers leaving the vessel under strict health protocols. These individuals boarded a chartered flight bound for the **Gómez Ulla Central Defense hospital** in Madrid, where they will undergo quarantine to monitor for any delayed symptoms. The process was described as orderly, with photographs showing passengers exiting the ship at Granadilla port.

In parallel, plans are in place for further evacuations. **Five French passengers** are scheduled for repatriation on the same day, followed by hospitalization for **72 hours** in France. The broader operation aims to clear most of the ship's passengers and crew, culminating in a final repatriation flight to Australia on Monday, May 11, 2026. The operator has indicated that the MV Hondius will make an additional stop in Tenerife before proceeding to Rotterdam, its home port, likely for thorough decontamination.

Spanish authorities, in coordination with international partners, are managing the logistics to ensure safe transport. This includes health screenings at the port and during flights, though no contradictions in reporting were noted— all sources align on the asymptomatic status and phased approach. This incident raises immediate concerns for cruise enthusiasts planning trips to Spain's coastal regions, particularly the Canary Islands. Travelers to [Tenerife](https://go2-spain.com/city/tenerife/) should note that port operations may face temporary delays or enhanced screenings, but no widespread travel restrictions have been announced for the island or broader Canary Islands.

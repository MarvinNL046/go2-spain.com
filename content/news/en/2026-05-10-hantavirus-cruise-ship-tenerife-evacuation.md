A Dutch-flagged cruise ship, the MV Hondius, carrying 140 passengers and crew and stricken by a hantavirus outbreak, is set to arrive in Tenerife, part of Spain's Canary Islands, early Sunday morning after weeks at sea. At least eight people have fallen ill, with three deaths reported and five known infections among those who disembarked earlier; Spanish authorities plan a fully isolated evacuation to prevent wider spread, amid low public health risks as confirmed by the World Health Organization.

## Outbreak Background

The outbreak on the MV Hondius, operated by Oceanwide Expeditions, was first detected on May 2, 2026. The ship has been stranded at sea since then, with health authorities on four continents now tracking more than two dozen passengers who left before the issue surfaced. These individuals, along with potential contacts, are under monitoring to contain any further transmission.

Hantavirus, typically spread through contact with contaminated rodent droppings, is not easily transmitted between humans. However, the specific Andes virus strain identified in this case may allow rare person-to-person spread. Symptoms appear between one and eight weeks after exposure, which explains the delayed detection on board. According to Al Jazeera, the cruise operator reported on Friday that no additional symptomatic cases were present among the remaining passengers and crew.

The World Health Organization (WHO) has emphasized that the overall risk to the public remains low. WHO spokesman **Christian Lindmeier** stated, “This is not a new COVID,” highlighting that the virus does not spread as readily from person to person. WHO Director-General **Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus** addressed concerns in a letter posted on X (formerly Twitter) to Tenerife residents on Saturday, acknowledging lingering fears from the 2020 pandemic but assuring that this situation differs significantly. He wrote, “You will not encounter [the passengers]. Your families will not encounter them.”

One WHO health officer, along with experts from the European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control and the Netherlands, is already aboard the ship providing support. Additionally, **Tedros** is scheduled to be on Tenerife to coordinate the response, as confirmed by Spanish ministry sources.

## Local Concerns and Passenger Fears

Residents in the Canary Islands, a popular destination off the west coast of Africa known for its beaches and volcanic landscapes—detailed in our [Canary Islands guide](https://go2-spain.com/canary-islands/)—have voiced worries about the ship's arrival. The Spanish anti-establishment group Iustitia Europa, which gained attention for challenging COVID-19 restrictions, demanded that the Hondius be barred from Spanish shores. In a post on X, the group stated, “The Canary Islands cannot become Europe’s health laboratory … We demand transparency, responsibility, and protection for Spaniards to avoid repeating the mistakes of the past.”

Alicia Rodriguez, a bar owner in Tenerife, told Al Jazeera that the vessel has been “the talk of the town” for days. She expressed moderate concern but hoped for careful handling to minimize dangers. Spanish passengers aboard the ship shared their anxieties with The Associated Press, fearing ostracism upon return. One unnamed passenger said, “We’re scared by all the news that’s coming out, by how people are going to receive us.” Another added, “You see what’s out there and you realise you’re heading into the eye of the hurricane … In reality, there are 140 human beings.”

These sentiments underscore the human impact beyond the medical facts, with evacuees feeling like unintended focal points of public scrutiny.

## Evacuation Protocols

Spain’s head of emergency services, **Virginia Barcones**, outlined that the 140 passengers and crew will be disembarked into a “completely isolated, cordoned-off area” upon arrival. The process begins with transport via small boats to shore for screening, as explained by **Maria Van Kerkhove**, WHO’s head of pandemic preparedness, during a Saturday media conference.

Symptomatic individuals will be transferred to the Netherlands for treatment, while asymptomatic passengers will board repatriation flights home. Spanish officials confirmed that evacuation to buses will only occur once flights are prepared, using isolated and guarded vehicles. A patient previously reported in intensive care in South Africa is now “doing better,” according to **Van Kerkhove**.

Civil guard officers have already inspected the Tenerife dock area, preparing for the controlled operation. This meticulous approach aims to ensure no contact with the local population, aligning with WHO guidelines.

## What This Means for Travelers

The hantavirus situation on the MV Hondius poses minimal risk to tourists in the Canary Islands or mainland Spain, given the contained evacuation and low transmissibility. Travelers should stay informed via official sources like the WHO or Spanish health authorities rather than social media speculation. Routine precautions, such as hand hygiene and avoiding rodent-prone areas, remain advisable for any trip—especially in natural settings like Tenerife's hikes or beaches.

No broader travel restrictions have been announced, but those with upcoming cruises or visits to the Canaries might monitor updates. The incident highlights the importance of health protocols in maritime travel, but experts stress it won't disrupt the region's vibrant tourism scene.

## What's Next

Following the Sunday arrival, the focus will shift to repatriation and contact tracing. Health officials will continue monitoring former passengers worldwide, with the WHO leading international coordination. Spanish authorities expect the operation to conclude swiftly, allowing Tenerife to return to normalcy.

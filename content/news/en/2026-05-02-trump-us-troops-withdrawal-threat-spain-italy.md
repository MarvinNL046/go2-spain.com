US President **Donald Trump** threatened on May 1, 2026, to withdraw American troops stationed in **Spain** and **Italy**, escalating tensions with NATO allies over their perceived lack of support in the ongoing US-Israeli war against Iran. The announcement, made during a public address, came a day after Trump indicated plans to reduce US military presence in **Germany**, citing criticism from European leaders and allies' failure to contribute naval forces to secure the Strait of Hormuz, a vital shipping route. This follows **Spain**'s Prime Minister **Pedro Sánchez**'s vocal opposition to the conflict from its outset and **Italy**'s recent refusal to allow US aircraft to use a Sicilian airbase for weapons transport related to the war.

## Background on the Escalating Tensions

The threats mark a significant strain in US-Europe relations, rooted in the broader context of the US-Israeli military actions against Iran. According to **The Guardian**, Trump has repeatedly criticized NATO members for not deploying their navies to help reopen the Strait of Hormuz, which has been disrupted amid the conflict. This corridor is crucial for global commercial shipping, and its partial closure has raised concerns about energy supplies and economic impacts worldwide.

In **Spain**, the US maintains a substantial military footprint, including naval bases in Rota and air facilities in Morón de la Frontera, hosting thousands of troops and supporting NATO operations. **Italy** hosts even larger contingents, with key installations like the naval base in Naples and airbases in Aviano and Sigonella, Sicily. The latter became a flashpoint when Rome, under Prime Minister **Giorgia Meloni**, declined in late March 2026 to permit US planes carrying weapons—intended for use in the Iran conflict—to operate from the Sicilian facility. Trump specifically remarked that "**Italy has not been of any help to us**," highlighting his frustration.

The German angle adds context: Chancellor **Friedrich Merz** recently stated that America was being "humiliated" by Iran, prompting Trump's initial threat to scale back troops there. **Spain**'s stance has been consistent, with **Sánchez** publicly opposing the war since it began, emphasizing diplomatic solutions over military escalation. **Italy**, meanwhile, had attempted a more balanced approach until the Sicily decision tipped the scales.

No official responses have emerged from **Meloni** or **Sánchez** as of the latest updates on May 1, though Italy's defense minister expressed surprise, stating, "**I don't understand the reasons**." This lack of immediate reaction underscores the diplomatic delicacy, as both nations rely on the US presence for security guarantees within NATO.

## Implications for Transatlantic Security and NATO

Trump's rhetoric signals a potential review of the entire US military posture in Europe, which could reshape NATO dynamics. The alliance, formed in 1949 to counter Soviet threats, now faces internal divisions over the Iran conflict. With approximately 35,000 US troops in **Germany**, 12,000 in **Italy**, and around 7,000 in **Spain**, any withdrawals would not only affect bilateral ties but also Europe's collective defense capabilities.

For **Spain**, the stakes are high. The Rota base, for instance, supports US Sixth Fleet operations in the Mediterranean and has been integral to counterterrorism efforts. A pullout could strain resources for joint exercises and surveillance missions. In **Italy**, the Sigonella base is pivotal for aerial refueling and intelligence gathering, and its denial already strained relations—Trump's threat amplifies that rift.

European leaders have navigated a tightrope: supporting NATO commitments while avoiding entanglement in the Middle East war. **Sánchez**'s criticism aligns with broader EU sentiments favoring de-escalation, while **Meloni**'s government, more aligned with conservative US views, still prioritized national sovereignty in the Sicily incident. The absence of naval contributions to the Hormuz effort reflects this caution, as European allies weigh domestic political costs against alliance obligations.

Speculation abounds about economic ripple effects, though details remain unclear. Reduced US presence might lead to job losses at bases and decreased local spending by troops, but these are not yet quantified. What's known is the political shockwave: Trump's comments revive debates from his first term about "fair share" contributions to NATO, where he accused allies of freeloading.

# Franco-Themed Cafes Thrive in Spain Despite Symbol Ban

Spain has officially banned Francoist symbols, yet a network of bars and restaurants across the country continues to openly glorify **Francisco Franco** and his dictatorship. These establishments—from roadside motorway stops to Madrid's city centre—display portraits of the dictator, play Francoist anthems, and sell memorabilia celebrating the regime, creating an unusual contradiction in how Spain grapples with its authoritarian past.

## A Living Museum to Dictatorship

The most prominent example is **Una Grande Libre** in Madrid's Usera neighbourhood, named after Franco's motto meaning "one, great, free." The bar features a large portrait of Franco superimposed on its window, a stone bust inside, and numerous photographs of the dictator throughout. Owner **Xiangwei Chen**, a Chinese immigrant, has become a minor public figure known as "el chino facha" (the Chinese fascist)—a moniker even appearing on wine bottles in his establishment. He has named his son Franco.

Other establishments include **El Cangrejo** in Ciudad Real and **Casa Pepe** in Despeñaperros, both decorated ostentatiously in Spanish flag colours with Falange symbols and the Eagle of San Juan. **El Rincón Nacional** in Ávila serves 1kg steaks on tables bearing Franco's portrait. At **Restaurante El Cangrejo**, staff have Photoshopped Franco into a Real Madrid shirt.

The atmosphere extends beyond décor. Diners may hear the Francoist anthem "Cara Al Sol" played repeatedly throughout the day. Owner **José Antonio Delgado** answers the phone with "Arriba, España"—another Francoist motto. Sugar packets commemorate the 1981 attempted military coup. **Casa Pepe** even operates an attached shop selling Franco-themed merchandise: tote bags bearing the dictator's face and tins of Spanish smoked paprika in Francoist flag designs.

## The Ruta 36 Pilgrimage

Many of these establishments are roadside bars situated on motorways, inspired by US-style highway culture. They form part of **Ruta 36**, a pilgrimage route named after both the highway and Franco's coup year of 1936. Some bars offer complimentary meals to visitors who collect stamps from each establishment along the route.

## The Contradiction with Recent Legislation

According to [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2026/may/18/spain-franco-symbols-cafes-dictator), these venues seem increasingly confusing given **Pedro Sánchez's recent historical memory legislation**, which banned Francoist symbols. The persistence of these cafes highlights what the article describes as Spain's "unique way that Spain deals with its past – or fails to."

The existence of these establishments raises fundamental questions about enforcement, interpretation, and Spain's relationship with its authoritarian history. While official symbols have been prohibited, these privately-owned businesses continue operating in a legal grey area, suggesting gaps between legislation and implementation.

## What This Means for Visitors

Travellers exploring Spain may encounter these Franco-themed establishments, particularly along motorway routes. They represent a distinctive—if unsettling—aspect of contemporary Spanish culture and the ongoing debate about historical memory. Understanding their existence provides insight into how different regions and individuals in Spain process the country's dictatorship era.

The contradiction between the symbol ban and these thriving venues underscores Spain's complex relationship with its past, making them notable cultural artifacts for those interested in modern European history and memory politics.

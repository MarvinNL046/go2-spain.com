NATO has stated there is no provision in its founding treaty to suspend or expel member states, in response to a report suggesting the United States might seek to punish **Spain** for not supporting its military campaign against Iran. The controversy emerged from an internal Pentagon email, as reported by Reuters and covered by the [BBC](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cz78x703lrvo), amid escalating tensions in the US-Iran war that began with US and Israeli attacks on Iran on 28 February 2026. Spain's refusal to allow the use of its air bases for strikes has drawn US criticism, raising questions about alliance unity.

### Background on the US-Iran Conflict and NATO Tensions

The current dispute stems from the ongoing war between the United States and Iran, which intensified after US and Israeli forces attacked Iranian targets on 28 February 2026. In retaliation, Iran restricted shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, a critical global oil route, prompting further US-led actions including a blockade of Iranian ports. **US President Donald Trump** has repeatedly criticized NATO allies for not contributing more to the effort, describing the 32-member alliance as a "one-way street" where the US protects others without reciprocal support.

Spain, a NATO member since 1982, has two major US military bases on its territory: Naval Station Rota and Morón Air Base. However, **Spanish Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez** has refused to permit their use for attacks on Iran, emphasizing that Spain's cooperation with allies must adhere to international law. This stance has frustrated US officials, who view it as insufficient support. A leaked internal Pentagon email, quoted by a US official to Reuters, outlined potential punitive measures against allies perceived as failing to meet "absolute baseline" commitments on access, basing, and overflight rights (ABO).

The email not only targeted Spain but also suggested reviewing the US position on the UK's claim to the Falkland Islands in the South Atlantic, which Argentina also claims. This broader approach indicates US frustration with European allies more generally. **US Defence Secretary Pete Hegseth** echoed these sentiments during a news conference on Friday, urging Europe to "stop talking and get a boat," noting that the continent relies heavily on the Strait of Hormuz for energy imports. He added that the era of "free riding" on US protection is over.

In contrast, the UK has provided more active support, allowing the US to use British bases for strikes and deploying RAF planes to shoot down Iranian drones. **UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer**, however, has stated that deeper involvement in the war or blockade is not in Britain's interest. France and other allies have expressed willingness to help keep the Strait open post-ceasefire.

### NATO's Official Response and Spain's Dismissal

A NATO official directly addressed the report, telling the BBC that the alliance's founding treaty "does not foresee any provision for suspension of NATO membership, or expulsion." This firm statement underscores the structural unity of the organization, designed during the Cold War to prevent such internal fractures. The response aims to quell speculation about the alliance's stability amid the Iran conflict.

Spain's government has downplayed the leaked email. **Pedro Sánchez** told reporters that decisions are based on "official documents and official positions," not internal communications. He reaffirmed Spain's commitment to "full co-operation with its allies, but always within the framework of international law." The Pentagon, through Press Secretary **Kingsley Wilson**, defended the US approach, stating that despite "everything" America has done for NATO partners, "they were not there for us." Wilson indicated that the defense department would provide the president with "credible options" to ensure allies contribute meaningfully, but offered no further details on internal deliberations.

**Pentagon Press Secretary Kingsley Wilson**'s comments highlight ongoing US exasperation, but they stop short of confirming any specific actions against Spain. Italian Prime Minister **Giorgia Meloni** weighed in at an EU summit, urging NATO members to "stick together" and strengthen the European pillar of the alliance to complement the American one, calling it a "source of strength."

### Implications for US-Spain Relations and the Alliance

This episode reveals deepening cracks within NATO, particularly over burden-sharing in conflicts outside traditional European defense. The US views Spain's base access as a cornerstone of alliance commitments, and any perceived withholding could strain bilateral ties. However, NATO's lack of expulsion mechanisms means any US efforts to "suspend" Spain would likely involve diplomatic pressure, reduced military cooperation, or bilateral repercussions rather than formal alliance action.

The mention of the Falklands in the email adds a layer of geopolitical complexity, potentially linking the Iran war to long-standing colonial disputes. While the US has historically supported the UK's position, reassessing it could signal broader retaliatory tactics. For Spain, the controversy reinforces its policy of multilateralism and legal constraints on military involvement, a position shared by other European nations wary of escalation in the Middle East.

No concrete actions have been announced, and the BBC has contacted the UK government for comment without response as of publication. The situation remains fluid, with speculation about US options tempered by NATO's structural safeguards.

# Sánchez Popular Abroad, but Spain's Voters Turn Away

**Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez enjoys admiration across Europe and North America for his forceful positions on Gaza and immigration, yet his Socialist Party (PSOE) suffered a historic defeat in Andalusia's regional elections on Sunday, signalling deepening domestic political trouble ahead of a 2027 general election.**

Internationally, **Sánchez** has earned respect for speaking out against **Benjamin Netanyahu** and **Donald Trump** earlier and more forcefully than many European counterparts, with a powerful message centred on international law. He has also become one of Europe's clearest advocates for immigration in one of the continent's fastest-growing nations. Most Spaniards support his outspoken positions on Israel-Palestine and his economic arguments for immigration, according to [The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2026/may/21/pedro-sanchez-loved-everywhere-not-so-much-spain-andalusia-voters-another-comeback).

Yet this international goodwill has not translated into domestic electoral success. The PSOE's loss in Andalusia marks the party's fourth regional election defeat in six months, a trajectory that suggests the socialists face an uphill battle in next year's general election.

## The Andalusia Collapse

The results in Andalusia are particularly devastating for the PSOE, which governed the region for nearly 40 years. The party's first prime minister after Franco's death, **Felipe González**, hailed from Seville, and the PSOE's national successes in the 1980s were deeply rooted in Andalusian support. On Sunday, **María Jesús Montero**, a former minister in Sánchez's government, delivered the party's worst result since Spain's democratic restoration, securing just 22.7% of the vote—approximately half the share the PSOE was securing in elections during the 2000s.

The beneficiary of the socialist collapse is not only their traditional rival, the centre-right Partido Popular (PP), but also the far-right Vox party. The PP's insufficient majority means it will require Vox support to govern Andalusia. Meanwhile, the leftist, regionalist Adelante Andalucía emerged as an unexpected winner, rising from two seats to eight.

## Mounting Scandals and Polling Decline

While local factors—such as the popularity of Andalusian president **Juanma Moreno** and voter resentment toward a PSOE candidate perceived as parachuted in from Madrid—partly explain the defeat, the result reflects broader weakening of Sánchez's national position.

The PP has led national polls for more than a year, buoyed by corruption scandals involving former socialist officials and even Sánchez's wife, who has been charged in a legal case brought by a far-right group. She denies any wrongdoing. This week, another major scandal emerged: a judge placed former prime minister **José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero** under investigation for alleged influence peddling. He also denies wrongdoing.

These cascading scandals have created a difficult political environment for the government, even as the PSOE's core policy positions enjoy public support.

## What's Next for Spanish Politics

With a general election scheduled for 2027, Sánchez faces a narrowing window to reverse the PSOE's electoral trajectory. The party's collapse in its traditional stronghold of Andalusia signals that international diplomatic success and popular economic policies may not be enough to overcome domestic political headwinds.

The contrast between Sánchez's standing abroad and his weakening position at home underscores the gap between Spain's international profile and its internal political divisions—a dynamic that will shape Spanish politics through the coming election cycle.
